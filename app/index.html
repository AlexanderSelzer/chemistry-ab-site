The pH scale

The pH scale represents the acidity or alkalinity of a substance. It represents a range between both extremes, the values 0 and 14, categorizing a substance as either acidic, when lower than 7, alkaline, when higher than 7, or neutral, when around the middle value of 7.

Values on the pH scale are logarithmic (base 10). Every step value approaching the extremes is ten times more acidic than the previous. A pH-value of 4 is 100 times more acidic than a value of 6.

    Natural Factors

    While strong changes in pH are often human-caused, the acidity or alkalinity of environments can vary in natural ways, too.

    Pine forests and other trees usually increase the acidity of their environment. Decomposing needles release acids into the environment, and at fall, falling leaves will lower the pH of water.

    Natural precipitation is slightly acidic, between the pH values of 5 and 6, due to Carbon Dioxide being dissolved.

    Calcium Carbonate helps keep the pH close to 7 by combining with the Hydrogen atoms and neutralizing it.

    In rarer events, volcanic erruptions may increase the acidity of water significantly.

    Human Factors

    Human Actions have the potential to greatly disturb the pH balance, which is found in the nature.

    Mining openly can be seen as a great acceleration of erosion. This type of erosion releases minerals into the ground water at much higher rates than they would be in nature. The minerals are often highly soluble and strongly alkaline or acidic, affecting the pH.
